Reflective Statement

How my writing changed across the semester

This reflection explains how my writing developed through audience awareness, genre, and revision.

When I began this class, I had a good amount of knowledge about what constitutes good writing, and I felt confident in my ability to recognize quality ideas and organize them into an essay. However, after completing the multigenre project, my perception of good writing changed significantly. At the beginning of the semester, I found the idea of one paper being read by different audiences for different purposes confusing. As we moved through the revision process, I began to understand the subtle but important differences between forms of writing and how those differences affect how an audience interprets an argument. Good writing doesn’t just happen—it requires intentional decisions about how the reader will experience the text.

I went back and read through my first draft outline for Project 1 and was really impressed by how far I have come in terms of writing and analytical skills throughout this semester. My thesis in my first draft was far too general and vague, but it served its initial purpose, which was to allow me to pick a topic and begin building ideas. At that point, I wasn’t writing to argue a point—I was writing to meet requirements. I didn’t consider audience, and as a result, I didn’t fully show my writing ability. Now I actively think about audience, which has made a huge difference in how I write.

My approach to organization also needed improvement. Although I spent time outlining my ideas, that organization did not translate into the final essay. Sections lacked direction and purpose. After reviewing feedback, I realized I needed to focus not just on structure, but on clarity, evidence, and engagement. I also began evaluating my work from a reader’s perspective instead of focusing only on what I intended to say.

Over time, my focus shifted. At the beginning of the semester, I wanted to include all my ideas. By the end, I focused on making sure the reader could understand and respond to those ideas. This meant improving structure, language, and choosing the right genre for the situation. I also learned that revision is more than correcting errors—it’s about rethinking how the message works.

My favorite part of the course was the multigenre campaign because it felt practical and allowed me to apply what I learned to real-world situations. Writing for civil engineers and infrastructure planners showed me how different audiences think. Engineers focus on systems and risk, while planners focus more on long-term implementation. This experience helped me understand how important it is to tailor writing to specific audiences.

I also learned how different genres function. The PSA flyer, infographic, and brochure each serve different purposes. The flyer grabs attention, the infographic organizes information, and the brochure builds a more detailed argument. This showed me that the same idea can be communicated in different ways, sometimes more effectively depending on the format.

During my global revision, I worked to make my writing more specific and connected to real-world applications. My instructor suggested that I better connect my strategies to engineering practice and clearly show where decisions happen in the design process. I also needed to better distinguish between audiences. This helped me move from describing ideas to making more persuasive arguments.

I revised my campaign by making it more practical. The PSA now includes clearer actions, the infographic connects risks to decisions, and the brochure treats cybersecurity as a design requirement. I also improved my rhetorical analysis by explaining why my choices were effective instead of just describing them.

During this process, I learned a lot about how important it is to choose the right words in my writing. For example, turning an unclear suggestion into a clear recommendation makes an idea much more useful and applicable. The use of different rhetorical strategies also helped me focus my writing toward specific groups. I liked being able to take an idea and turn it into something practical for the reader. As I saw how careful language and rephrasing could make a complicated idea easier to understand, I realized how important it is to be clear and precise in technical writing.

One of my biggest improvements was in rhetorical analysis. Before, I focused on describing what a text does. Now, I explain how and why those choices influence the audience. Asking “why does this matter?” helped me develop stronger analysis.

I also learned that revision involves rethinking purpose, audience, and structure. It requires stepping back and making meaningful changes, not just surface edits. This process was challenging but improved my writing significantly.

Overall, my writing progressed from general summaries to more focused, audience-aware, and analytical writing. I learned that different situations require different approaches and that there is no single best way to write.

In the future, I plan to focus on making clear arguments, choosing appropriate formats, and writing with my audience in mind. I will also treat revision as an essential part of the writing process rather than an afterthought.

I also want to work on changing my tone and level of detail based on who I am writing for in the future. Technical audiences usually expect writing to be clear, direct, and efficient, while other audiences may need more explanation or background. Being able to adjust my writing in this way will help me communicate more effectively and present my ideas in a way that is both clear and appropriate for the situation.

Looking at my portfolio, I see steady growth rather than a sudden change. I have become more aware of audience, purpose, and revision, and more capable of writing effective arguments.
